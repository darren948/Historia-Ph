Tabon Cave

About Tabon Cave

The Tabon Caves is a cave system located in Lipuun Point, Panitian, Quezon, Palawan in the Philippines. Dubbed as the country's "cradle of civilization", it is a site of archaeological importance due to the number of jar burials and prehistoric human remains found starting from the 1960s, most notably the Tabon Man. The system is a part of the Lipuun Point Reservation, which has been protected by the Philippine government as a museum reservation to protect the caves and its immediate vicinity from deforestation and to preserve the cultural artiFACTs present there.

FACT


Earliest cave uses included inhabitation, and the Factory like production of stone tools. According to a video by Dr. Fox, a jar burial period began 3000 years ago and lasted until 1500 years ago. This is evidence the area became dominated by people known as the Sa Huỳnh culture. The Sa Huynh adorned their dead with agate, carnelian, and glass beads from India and Iran. Artifact of this nature, including glass bracelets, were found in the cave and are displayed at the Palawan Cultural Museum in Puerto Princesa. A migration of Sa Huyhn people to Vietnam is accepted by most experts to have been the forebears of the Cham people. These are a people still existing today that created an empire known as Champa. History of the people in the area took on a new significance in modern times owing to the Spratly Islands dispute.

INFORMATION


The caves are named after the Tabon scrubfowl. It is bordered on the south by the town proper of Quezon, Panitian on the west, and the South China Sea on the north and east. Out of 215 known caves, 29 have been fully explored, with seven of them publicly accessible. Other excavated, unexamined remains are stored onsite. In 2006, the site, collectively named as Tabon Cave Complex and all of Lipuun, was added to the tentative list of the Philippines for future UNESCO World Heritage Site nomination.

Spanish Colonization (1565 - 1898)

About 1565 - 1898

The history of the Philippines from 1565 to 1898 is known as the Spanish colonial period, during which the Philippine Islands were ruled as the Captaincy General of the Philippines within the Spanish East Indies, initially under the Viceroyalty of New Spain, based in Mexico City, until the independence of the Mexican Empire from Spain in 1821. This resulted in direct Spanish control during a period of governmental instability there.

FACT


1. 1521 - Portuguese explorer Ferdinand Magellan, sailing under the Spanish flag, arrives in the Philippines and claims the islands for Spain. He is killed at the Battle of Mactan by Lapu-Lapu, the chieftain of Mactan Island, marking the first resistance against Spanish colonization.
2. 1565 - Miguel López de Legazpi arrives and establishes the first Spanish settlement in Cebu, beginning formal Spanish colonization.
3. 1571 - Legazpi conquers Manila, which becomes the capital of the Spanish Philippines and a central hub for trade and government. Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade starts, linking the Philippines to Mexico and Spain and making Manila a significant trade center for over two centuries.

1. Missionary Efforts - Spanish friars and missionaries begin converting indigenous populations to Christianity. The Spanish build numerous churches, which serve as both religious and community centers, helping to spread Catholicism, which becomes deeply rooted in Filipino culture.
2. Political Organization - The Spanish divide the country into provinces and towns (pueblos) and establish the encomienda system, where local leaders are given control over land and people. The system eventually transitions to haciendas, large agricultural estates often controlled by Spanish landlords

Philippine Revolution

About Philippine Revolution

Filipino independence struggle that, after more than 300 years of Spanish colonial rule, exposed the weakness of Spanish administration but failed to evict Spaniards from the islands. The Spanish-American War brought Spain’s rule in the Philippines to a close in 1898 but precipitated the Philippine-American War, a bloody war between Filipino revolutionaries and the U.S. Army.

FACT



1. By 1892 it became obvious that Spain was unwilling to reform its colonial government. Andres Bonifacio, a self-educated warehouse clerk, organized a secret revolutionary society, the Katipunan, in Manila
2. In March 1897 leadership of the revolution passed to a young general, Emilio Aguinaldo, who had Bonifacio shot for alleged sedition.
3. On December 15, 1897, the pact of Biak-na-Bato was proclaimed. Though its precise terms have been a matter of impassioned debate ever since, the pact brought a temporary end to the Philippine Revolution

INFORMATION


1. The Philippine Revolution took place from 1896 to 1898
2. The Katipunan, a secret society led by Andrés Bonifacio, organized the revolution. Bonifacio is often called "The Father of the Philippine Revolution".
3. The revolution's goals were to create an independent Philippines and promote nationalist goals of freedom, independence, and equality.

American Colonization

About American Colonization

The history of the Philippines from 1898 to 1946 is known as the American colonial period, and began with the outbreak of the Spanish–American War in April 1898

FACT


1. Crisis Phase (December 10, 1898-October 31, 1899): The United States government formally acquired the Philippines from Spain with the signing of the Treaty of Paris on December 10, 1898
2. Conflict Phase (November 1, 1899-April 13, 1902): Emilio Aguinaldo led a rebellion against the U.S. military government in the Philippines beginning on November 1, 1899.
3. Post-Conflict Phase (April 17, 1902-June 15, 1913): U.S. government troops commanded by Colonel Frank Baldwin clashed with Moros near Bayan on the island of Mindanao on May 2, 1902, resulting in the deaths of some 350 Moros and eleven U.S. soldiers. The U.S. Congress approved the Philippines Act on July 1, 1902, which provided the Philippines with limited self-government
4. Post-Crisis Phase (June 16, 1913-December 7, 1941): Francis Harrison was sworn in as U.S. governor of the Philippines on August 21, 1913. Legislative elections were held on June 6, 1916, and the Nationalist Party (Partido Nacionalista,- PN) won 75 out of 90 seats in the House of Representatives
5. Crisis Phase (December 8, 1941-October 17, 1945): Japanese military aircraft attacked the U.S. government’s Clark airfield in the Philippines on December 8, 1941, resulting in the deaths of 80 U.S. military personnel.

INFORMATION


1. The Spanish–American War began in April 1898, and the Treaty of Paris was signed in December 1898, ceding the Philippines to the United States.
2. The Philippine–American War was a brutal conflict that lasted from 1899–1902. The war resulted in many civilian deaths, and the U.S. military government implemented policies that included burning villages, civilian reconcentration, and torture.
3. Reforms - The U.S. established a civil government and implemented reforms in areas such as education, health, and the economy.
4. Independence - The U.S. promised to withdraw at the end of the transition period, which included the constitution of a Commonwealth from 1935–1946. The Philippines formally gained independence from the U.S. on July 4, 1946

Post-Independence Era

Post-Independence Era

The 4th of July used to be considered an important national holiday in the Philippines. Not because it was the United States’ birthday, but because it was Philippine Independence Day in 1946. Seventy five years ago, the Philippines was recognized as an independent, sovereign country by the United States, which withdrew its authority over the archipelago as colonizer.

FACT


1. The Philippines had been a Spanish colony since 1565, and since that time numerous revolts broke out challenging Spanish rule. These revolts were disunited, however, until the nineteenth century when nationalism brought forth a more united anti-colonial movement. This culminated in a revolution that broke out in 1896. After much fighting, a stalemate ensued, leading to a ceasefire agreement between Filipino and Spanish leaders.
2. Quezon, the dominant political leader in the Philippines at that time, believed he could influence the new American president, Franklin D. Roosevelt, and the Democratic congress to rectify his main objections in a new Philippine independence bill.
3. Prior to the establishment of the Philippine Commonwealth, a constitution had to be drafted. A constitutional convention was thus elected, and finished its draft in February 1935.
4. It was approved in a nationwide plebiscite, and national elections for the new government were held in September 1935.
5. The Philippine Commonwealth was formally inaugurated on November 15, 1935, an unprecedented world event in which the United States, a colonial power, was preparing to let go of its colony. The ramifications were keenly felt among other colonial governments and colonized people.

INFORMATION


1. Reconstruction - The United States assisted in the reconstruction of the Philippines after the war.
2. Huk Rebellion - A communist-inspired rebellion that complicated the recovery efforts from 1945–1953. President Ramon Magsaysay was able to successfully suppress the rebellion.
3. Domestic reform - Presidents Carlos P. Garcia and Diosdado Macapagal implemented domestic reform programs
4. President Ferdinand E. Marcos declared martial law in 1972, citing growing lawlessness and rebellion. He ruled by decree and popular referenda, suppressing democratic institutions and civil liberties
5. The People Power Revolution drove Marcos from power and led to the presidency of Corazon Aquino.

Marcelo H. del Pilar

About Marcelo Hilario del Pilar y Gatmaitán

Del Pilar was one of the leading figures of the Propaganda Movement, which aimed to bring reforms to the Philippines under Spanish colonial rule.

FACT


1. He became the editor of La Solidaridad in 1889, the propaganda newspaper that became the primary voice for reformists seeking political and social change in the Philippines.
2. As a writer and editor, del Pilar used La Solidaridad to call for reforms such as the representation of Filipinos in the Spanish Cortes (parliament), the freedom of the press, and the removal of the Spanish friars’ excessive influence over Filipino affairs.
3. Del Pilar fiercely opposed the dominance of the Spanish clergy in the governance of the Philippines. He critiqued their control over political and educational systems, and promoted the separation of Church and State, a core belief that would later influence the goals of the Philippine Revolution.

INFORMATION


1. His works helped form the intellectual basis for the Philippine Revolution, influencing key figures like José Rizal.
2. Through his writings, del Pilar inspired a sense of nationalism and the desire for Philippine independence.
3. A prominent member of the Freemasonry, del Pilar believed in the ideals of liberty, equality, and fraternity, and used these principles to unite Filipinos in their fight for independence.

Emilio Aguinaldo

About Emilio Aguinaldo y Famy

He was a key figure in the Philippine Revolution, the first president of the Philippines, and a leader of the Katipunan during the struggle against Spanish rule

FACT


1. Aguinaldo played a central role in the Philippine Revolution and was the first president of the newly established Republic of the Philippines. He declared Philippine independence on June 12, 1898, in Cavite
2. His declaration of independence remains a significant moment in Philippine history. Despite later controversies surrounding his leadership

INFORMATION


1. Declaration of Philippine Independence - Emilio Aguinaldo declared the Philippines' independence from Spanish colonial rule in Kawit, Cavite. This momentous event marked the birth of the First Philippine Republic
2. The Philippine-American War - Following the Spanish-American War, the Philippines was ceded to the United States, and Aguinaldo led the Filipino forces in the Philippine-American War.
3. Aguinaldo’s Capture and Subsequent Oath of Allegiance to the U.S. - Aguinaldo was captured by American forces in Palanan, Isabela. He swore an oath of allegiance to the United States, signaling the end of the armed resistance but later became an advocate for Philippine independence within the American-controlled government
